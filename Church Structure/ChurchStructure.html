Christ: The divine head and ultimate authority of the church, whose leadership is inherent and eternal. His role as the foundation of all spiritual governance is attested in Ephesians 1:22 and Colossians 1:18.

Apostles: The foundational leaders directly commissioned by Jesus Christ. Tasked with establishing the church and spreading the gospel, their unique mission and authority are detailed in Ephesians 2:20 and Acts 1:2-26. The title of Apostle is currently defunct since death of John in 100 AD.

Prophets: Those divinely called to receive and communicate God’s revelations. They offer guidance, correction, and insight to the church, as referenced in 1 Corinthians 12:28 and Ephesians 4:11.

Local Church: An autonomous assembly of believers formed under the guidance of the Holy Spirit. Its self-governing structure reflects the early church model found in Acts 14:23.

Elders: Mature, spiritually seasoned leaders responsible for governing, teaching, and shepherding the local assembly. Their qualifications and roles are outlined in 1 Timothy 3:1-7 and Titus 1:5-9.

Evangelists: Ministers gifted in proclaiming the gospel and nurturing faith through public witness. Their calling and work are affirmed in Ephesians 4:11 and Acts 21:8.

Pastors: Spiritual shepherds tasked with caring for, guiding, and instructing the congregation. Their role in protecting and nurturing the flock is highlighted in Ephesians 4:11 and 1 Peter 5:2.

Teachers: Instructors dedicated to elucidating biblical doctrine and practical Christian living, thereby fostering spiritual growth. Their contribution is recognized in Ephesians 4:11 and Acts 13:1.

Deacons: Leaders appointed to manage the practical and administrative needs of the church, ensuring effective service and support for the community. This role is outlined in 1 Timothy 3:8-13 and Acts 6:1-6.

Widows: Members who receive special support from the church and contribute through acts of care and hospitality, reflecting the community’s commitment to compassion as noted in 1 Timothy 5:9-10.

Members: All baptized believers actively participating in the life and mission of the church, contributing their unique gifts to the body of Christ as described in 1 Corinthians 12.

Pope: The Bishop of Rome, successor of Saint Peter, and supreme Pontiff of the universal Church. Appointed: Elected by the College of Cardinals in conclave. Authority Source: CCC 882–884, Code of Canon Law (Can. 331).

Cardinals: Senior church leaders who assist the Pope by providing counsel, governing the Church alongside him, and electing his successor in conclave.
Appointed: Personally chosen and appointed by the Pope, often from among bishops worldwide.
Authority Source: Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 874, 877), Code of Canon Law (Can. 349–359).

Archbishops: Successors to the Apostles who oversee provinces and manage multiple dioceses.
Appointed: Appointed by the Pope following a discernment process.
Authority Source: Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 877, 886), Code of Canon Law (Can. 375–411).

Bishops: Successors to the Apostles who are responsible for teaching doctrine, governing Catholics within their dioceses, sanctifying the faithful through sacraments, and ordaining new priests and deacons.
Appointed: Appointed by the Pope following a discernment process.
Authority Source: Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 877, 886, 1555–1561), Code of Canon Law (Can. 375–411).

Priests: Ordained ministers who administer the sacraments, preach the gospel, provide pastoral care, and lead parish communities under the bishop’s authority.
Appointed: Ordained by bishops after appropriate theological formation.
Authority Source: Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 1562–1568, 1592), Code of Canon Law (Can. 515–552).

Deacons: Ordained ministers who assist bishops and priests, proclaim the Gospel, preach homilies, administer baptism, witness marriages, and perform charitable works.
Appointed: Ordained by bishops after appropriate formation.
Authority Source: Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 1569–1571, 1596), Code of Canon Law (Can. 1008–1009).

Religious Orders: Communities of men and women living according to specific spiritual charisms and vows (poverty, chastity, obedience), contributing uniquely to the Church's mission through prayer, education, healthcare, and evangelization.
Appointed: Members voluntarily profess vows after a period of formation.
Authority Source: Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 914–933), Code of Canon Law (Can. 573–746).

Laity: Baptized Catholics who participate actively in the Church’s mission primarily in secular contexts, bearing witness to Christ in family life, society, and professional environments. They are neither ordained nor members of religious communities.
Appointed: Incorporated through baptism and confirmation.
Authority Source: Catechism of the Catholic Church (CCC 897–913), Code of Canon Law (Can. 204–231).

Christ: The sole and supreme head of the church, recognized as having absolute spiritual authority over the community of believers. (Colossians 1:18)

Bible: The inspired Word of God and the ultimate authority guiding all matters of faith, practice, and church governance, central to Baptist life and identity. (2 Timothy 3:16-17)

Congregation: The local body of believers who govern the church democratically, with each member having an equal voice in decisions. (Acts 6:3-5)

Pastor: In the Baptist tradition, the pastor is a spiritual leader called by the local congregation to preach, teach, provide pastoral care, and guide the church in mission and spiritual growth. The pastor serves under the congregation's authority and oversight, embodying the principle of the priesthood of all believers.

Deacons: Servant-leaders assisting the pastor and congregation in practical ministry and service roles. (Acts 6:1-6)

Committees: Groups established by the congregation to manage specific responsibilities such as finances, missions, education, worship, and community engagement, enabling organized and effective church operations. This structure reflects the Baptist emphasis on congregational governance and the priesthood of all believers.

Members: All baptized believers with equal voice in church decisions, actively participating in the church’s life and mission. (1 Corinthians 12:12-27)

Association: Voluntary regional fellowship of Baptist churches for mutual support and cooperation. (No binding authority over local churches)

Convention: Larger cooperative body for missions and education with no authority over local churches, reflecting Baptist autonomy. (Voluntary participation)

New Testament Church Structure

This chart reflects the biblical model where Christ is the head (Ephesians 1:22). Apostles and Prophets are foundational, equipping the early church (Ephesians 2:20, 4:11).

Each Local Assembly is autonomous (Acts 14:23), led by Elders (1 Timothy 3:1-7) who oversee and teach, often including Pastors (1 Peter 5:2).

Evangelists spread the gospel (Acts 21:8), and Teachers instruct (Acts 13:1). Deacons serve practical needs (Acts 6:1-6), and Widows are supported while serving (1 Timothy 5:9-10).

Members are all believers, active in the body (1 Corinthians 12). This community-focused structure emphasizes service under Christ's authority.

The Roman Catholic Church follows a hierarchical structure with the Pope as the supreme head, successor to St. Peter.

Cardinals serve as the Pope's principal counselors and elect new Popes. Archbishops oversee provinces and Bishops lead dioceses.

Priests administer sacraments and lead parishes, assisted by Deacons.

Religious Orders form communities dedicated to specific charisms and ministries. The Laity comprises all baptized members who aren't ordained clergy.

Baptist churches emphasize Christ as head and the Bible as the primary authority, with a distinctive congregational polity.

The Congregation governs democratically with each member having an equal vote. The Pastor leads and teaches while Deacons serve as ministry leaders.

Committees handle specific functions within the church. Associations and Conventions are voluntary fellowships with no authority over local churches.

Baptist churches emphasize local church autonomy, priesthood of all believers, and voluntary cooperation.
